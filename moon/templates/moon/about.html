LunaVis

Luna Vis is a crescent Moon visibility app. As well as giving you the daily Moon data for your selected location and date, Luna Vis allows you to determine when the new crescent Moon will be visible from your selected location.

Usability

Select a Location

Use the location icon in the navigation bar to select a location. Simply enter a place name and click ‘Find place’ or select the 'Use my location' button. Once you have selected your location the average elevation will automatically be determined. You can manually adjust this if you know the elevation of your specific location. If you are registered and logged in, you can add a location to your personal list of favourite locations.

Select a Date

The default date is for today’s date. To change the date, use the calendar icon in the navigation bar. You can also use the arrow keys to scroll through dates for up to &pm 16 days from the set date.

Daily Moon Data

The Daily Moon Data page shows core lunar and solar values for the selected location on the selected date at the current time.

New Moon Visibility

The New Moon visibility page shows the date for the next new Moon from the selected date. If the date is within 5 days of the new Moon it will show the information for that new Moon. The conjunction time is shown along with the consensus visibility – the first date that the crescent Moon is predicted to be visible, by all criteria methods - for the selected location. You can also use the arrow keys to scroll through months to see information for the previous and next new Moons. The 5-day visibility window is also shown giving more detailed information for each visibility criteria.

Observation Log

If you are registered and logged in you can use the Observation page to log your observations of the new Moon and contribute to citizen science. This data will be added to the database and used to constrain the prediction criteria.

The Science

The new Moon occurs when the Moon is positioned directly between the Earth and the Sun, such that the Moon is obscured from view by observers on Earth. The moment when this happens is exact and does not depend on the observer's location. We refer to this as the moment of conjunction, or conjunction time.

As the Moon continues in its orbit around the Earth, and the Earth continues in its orbit around the Sun, the face of the Moon slowly becomes illuminated. However, during this phase the sun and Moon are in the same part of the sky such that they rise and set at around the same time. So, although the relative position of the Sun and Moon is such that the illuminated face of the Moon would render a crescent, it may be obscured by the Sun's glare.

To determine when the new crescent Moon is first visible, we therefore need to consider many factors. Fortunately for us this has been studied since the time of the Babylonians and was furthered by many Muslim scientists such as: Yaʿqūb ibn Ṭāriq (Late 8th century); Al-Khwarizmi (780 - 850); Al-Farghani (800 - 870); Thābit ibn Qurra (826 – 901); Al-Battani (858 – 929); Abu Ja'far al-Khazin (900 - 971); Al-Biruni (973 – 1050); Nasir al-Din al-Tusi (1201 – 1274) and Al-Kashani (1380 - 1429).

The main criteria considered today are:

  1. Moons age: the time between conjunction and the time of observation.
  2. Lag time: The time between sunset and moonset.
  3. Moons Altitude: The angular distance of the Moon above the horizon.
  4. The arc of vision (ARCV): The angular difference in altitude between the Sun and the Moon. This is the vertical separation between the Sun and the Moon, relative to the horizon, and thus depends on the observer's location, elevation, and the time of day.
  5. Arc of light (ARCL): the angular separation between the Sun and the Moon, referred to as elongation. Elongation is invariant to the rotation of the observer's horizon and is thus not affected by the observers location.
  6. Relative azimuth (DAZ): The angular difference in azimuth between the Sun and the Moon. Note: the azimuth is azimuth is a horizontal angle measured clockwise in degrees from a north reference line (usually true north) to a target or celestial body, ranging from 0° to 360°
  7. Topocentric Crescent width (W): The angular width of the illuminated area of the Moon measured along the Moon's diameter. The topocentric width is calculated relative to the Earths surface and thus is dependent on the observers location and elevation.

Visibility Criteria

In this app we currently consider the criteria as developed by Yallop (1997) and Odeh (2006), but we plan to add more criteria in the future.

Yallop

Yallops (1997)criteria is based on the Indian method and was determined from a standard set of 295 first sightings of the new crescent Moon that cover the period 1859 to 1996. The visibility index, q, is calculated from the arc of vision (ARCV) and the topocentric crescent width, W, in arcminutes:

$$ q = ARCV - \left(11.8371 - 6.3226W + 0.7319W^2 - 0.1018W^3\right) $$

The value for q is then used to determine the visibility of the crescent Moon as follows:

  • $0.216 < q$Zone A, Easily visible ($ARCL \geq 12^\circ$).
  • $-0.014 < q \leq 0.216$Zone B, Visible under perfect conditions.
  • $-0.160 < q \leq -0.014$Zone C, May need optical aid to find crescent.
  • $-0.232 < q \leq -0.160$Zone D, Will need optical aid to find crescent.
  • $-0.293 < q \leq -0.232$Zone E, Not visible with a telescope ($ARCL \leq 8.5^\circ$).
  • $q \leq -0.293$Zone F, Not visible, below Danjon limit ($ARCL \leq 8.5^\circ$).

Note: The Danjon limit is the minimum elongation at which the crescent Moon can be seen, which is approximately 7°. However, Yallop's criterion suggests that the visibility limit is around 8.5°, which is slightly more conservative than the traditional Danjon limit.

Odeh

Odeh's (2006) criteria was established based on 737 observations and determines visibility from the topocentric arc of vision (ARCV) and the topocentric crescent width, W, in arcminutes. The visibility index, V, is calculated as follows:

$$ V = ARCV - \left(-0.1018W^3 + 0.7319W^2 - 6.3226W + 7.1651\right) $$

The value for V is then used to determine the visibility of the crescent Moon as follows:

  • $5.65$ \leq VZone A, Crescent is visible by naked eyes.
  • $2 \leq V < 5.65$Zone B, Crescent is visible by optical aid, and it could be seen by naked eyes.
  • $-0.96 \leq V < 2$Zone C, Crescent is visible by optical aid only.
  • $V < -0.96$Zone D, Crescent is not visible even by optical aid.
